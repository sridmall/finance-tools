### 3.1 Decision for AY 2026-27 (the return you're filing now)

Corporate NPS cannot be retrofitted for a year that already ended. The live choice is **A/B (old) vs C (new)**.

**→ File under the New Regime (Scenario C).** It beats the best achievable old-regime position (B) by **~₹64,900**, and since ₹22,81,083 was already withheld as TDS, filing new converts to a refund of **~₹1,33,532** (vs. only ~₹68,639 if you filed old with the corrected home-loan claim). Salaried individuals may choose their regime at the time of filing regardless of what the employer used for TDS.

### 3.2 Decision for FY 2026-27 onward (the live year)

**→ New regime + 14% Corporate NPS (Scenario E)** is the optimum: **~₹2,36,355/yr** better than your current as-filed position, and **~₹77,000/yr** better than the best old-regime build (D) even if you had set up 10% NPS there.

### 3.3 Why New wins despite your sizeable Old-regime deduction stack

Your old-regime deductions total **~₹6.6L/yr** (HRA ₹2.49L + Sec 24 ₹2L + 80C ₹1.5L + 80D ₹0.09L + potential 80CCD(1B) ₹0.5L) — not trivial. But at your income level, three New-regime structural advantages outweigh it:

1. **Wider, lower slabs** — more income taxed at 5–20% instead of jumping to 30% past ₹10L.
2. **Higher employer-NPS ceiling** — 80CCD(2) allows **14% of basic** in the new regime vs **10%** in the old.
3. **Bigger standard deduction** — ₹75,000 vs ₹50,000.

You are giving up HRA, Sec 24, 80C, 80D, and 80CCD(1B) entirely — the comparison above already prices that trade-off in, and New still comes out ahead by a comfortable margin.

## 4. Recommended plan: New Regime + Corporate NPS

### 4.1 Levers that survive in the New regime — use these

| Lever | Mechanism | Value to you |
|---|---|---|
| **80CCD(2) — Corporate/Employer NPS** | Employer contributes up to **14% of basic** (~₹3.0L/yr) to your NPS Tier-1; fully deductible, no cap under old-regime's 10%/₹1.5L-clubbed limit | ~₹1.03L/yr tax saved at your 34.32% marginal rate |
| **Standard deduction** | ₹75,000, automatic | Built into Scenario C/E |
| **Retirals-cap discipline** | Keep employer EPF + NPS + superannuation **combined under ₹7.5L/yr** (Sec 17(2)(vii)); above that, the excess becomes a taxable perquisite, and notional annual accretion on it is taxed under 17(2)(viia) | Avoids clawing back the NPS benefit |
| **Surcharge-cliff monitoring** | Your total income (~₹73.6L) sits in the 10% surcharge band (₹50L–1cr). Because your **RSU perquisite recurs each year**, a large vest could push you past ₹1cr, where old-regime surcharge rises to 15% (new regime caps at 25% total, no 37% tier) | Consider timing/spreading vests if approaching ₹1cr |

### 4.2 Levers you are giving up (confirm you're comfortable losing these)

HRA (₹2.49L), Sec 24 self-occupied home-loan interest (₹2L), 80C (₹1.5L), 80D (₹8,968), 80CCD(1B) (₹50K potential). All already netted into the Scenario C/E numbers above — New still wins net of losing these.

### 4.3 Beyond salary — recurring foreign-RSU tax hygiene (regime-independent)

Your RSU perquisite recurs annually and Cisco is NYSE-listed, so the *investment-income* side of your RSUs needs separate handling regardless of which salary regime you pick. Follow the existing framework in this repo, [`stock investing frameworks/foreign_stock_income_tax_filing.md`](stock%20investing%20frameworks/foreign_stock_income_tax_filing.md):

- RSU vesting is already taxed as salary perquisite (the ₹23.76L) — verify it's correctly reflected each year.
- On **sale**, compute capital gains as `Sale price − vesting FMV` (Sec 49(2AA)); foreign shares are **unlisted** for Indian tax purposes → Sec 112, 24-month LTCG cutoff, 12.5% LTCG without indexation; STCG at slab rate.
- File **Form W-8BEN** with your US broker (25% DTAA-capped dividend withholding instead of 30%).
- Claim **Foreign Tax Credit** via **Form 67 + Schedule FSI/TR** for US tax withheld on dividends.
- Disclose foreign holdings in **Schedule FA** (calendar-year basis, Tables A2/A3) — mandatory even if unsold, to avoid Black Money Act penalties.
- Use listed-Indian-equity tax-loss harvesting and the (regime-independent) ₹1.25L/yr Sec 112A LTCG exemption on domestic holdings to reduce investment-side tax further — this is separate from and additive to the salary-regime decision.
