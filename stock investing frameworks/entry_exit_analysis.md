## 1. The Complete Entry Protocol — Step by Step

Here is your refined, rules-based entry process from idea to first tranche.

### Step 1: Idea Generation (Where Do You Find Stocks?)

| Source | Method |
|---|---|
| Sectoral thesis | Identify macro tailwinds (e.g., EMS, solar, data centres, financialization) → find companies riding the wave |
| Screener filters | Screener.in: Revenue growth > 15%, ROCE > 15%, D/E < 1, Promoter holding > 40% |
| Concall discoveries | Listen to concalls of companies you own; their management often mentions competitors or suppliers worth investigating |
| Portfolio companies' supply chain | If Company A in your portfolio is doing well, who supplies to them? Who are their customers? |
| Peer comparison | If KAYNES is in your portfolio, have you evaluated Syrma SGS, Avalon Technologies, Centum Electronics? |

### Step 2: First-Pass Screening (5-Minute Check)

Run these quick checks on Screener.in / Trendlyne before investing any time:

| Check | Pass Criteria | Fail = Reject |
|---|---|---|
| Market Cap | > ₹500 Cr (avoid micro-caps) | Below ₹500 Cr |
| Revenue Growth (3yr CAGR) | > 12% | Below 5% |
| Profit Growth (3yr CAGR) | > 15% | Negative or below 10% |
| ROCE (latest year) | > 15% | Below 10% |
| Promoter Pledging | < 10% | Above 25% |
| Debt/Equity | < 1.0 | Above 2.0 |

If the stock passes all 6, proceed to Step 3. If it fails 2+, reject.

### Step 3: Business Quality Analysis (1-2 Hours)

**Apply the complete Fundamental Analysis Framework from `fundamental_analysis.md`:**

- P&L Analysis (5 years)
- OPM Expansion (8 quarters)
- Balance Sheet Analysis
- Cash Flow Analysis
- Financial Ratios
- Shareholding Pattern
- Corporate Governance Filter — must score >= 13/21 (see `quarterly_earnings_concall_analysis.md` Section 12)
- Earnings & Concall Analysis — last 2-3 quarters (see `quarterly_earnings_concall_analysis.md`)
- Brokerage Research

**The stock must score >= 70/100 on the Business Quality Scorecard.**

### Step 4: Valuation Analysis (30 Minutes)

Apply the Valuation Scorecard from `valuation_analysis.md`. The stock must score >= 65/100.

### Step 5: Write Your Investment Thesis

**Before deploying the first rupee, write down WHY you are buying this stock.** Use the Investment Thesis Template from `fundamental_analysis.md` Section 11.4.

This is the single most important discipline a long-term investor can develop.

**Why this matters:** When the stock falls 30% and your emotions scream "sell everything," you go back to this thesis. If the thesis is still intact, you accumulate more. If the thesis is broken, you exit without emotion.

### Step 6: Deploy First Tranche

Only after completing Steps 1-5 do you invest. The first tranche is your "scout" — a small position that gives you skin in the game and motivates you to track the company closely.

## 2. The Accumulation Protocol — Building Position in Tranches

### 2.1 Your Enhanced Tranche Strategy

Your current 5 tranches of ₹25,000 is a solid foundation. Here is the enhanced version with specific triggers for each tranche.

#### Standard Accumulation (5 Tranches of ₹25,000 = ₹1,25,000 Total)

| Tranche | Amount | Trigger | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **T1 — Scout** | ₹25,000 | Passes all screening + quality + valuation checks. Current quarter earnings are strong | After completing the Entry Protocol (Steps 1-5) |
| **T2 — Confirmation** | ₹25,000 | Next quarterly results confirm growth momentum. Management guidance reiterated or raised | After Q+1 earnings + concall analysis |
| **T3 — Conviction Builder** | ₹25,000 | Second consecutive strong quarter. Business thesis strengthening | After Q+2 earnings + concall analysis |
| **T4 — Maturation** | ₹25,000 | Third quarter validates. Stock may have appreciated but growth trajectory supports higher prices | After Q+3 earnings + concall analysis |
| **T5 — Completion** | ₹25,000 | Annual report confirms full-year performance. Governance checks clean | After annual report review |

**Key insight:** You are deploying ₹1,25,000 over approximately 12-15 months (4-5 quarters). This means you are validating the business across an entire annual cycle before completing your position. This is the discipline of a true long-term investor.

#### Important Tranche Rules

1. **Never skip ahead:** Do not deploy T3 before T2 just because the stock is "cheap." Each tranche earns the right to deploy through business performance validation.

2. **Any tranche can be the last:** If the Q+1 results are poor, T2 never deploys. You hold T1 and wait. If the next quarter is also poor, you reassess the entire thesis.

3. **Do not anchor to your entry price:** If you deployed T1 at ₹500 and the stock rises to ₹600 before T2 triggers, deploy T2 at ₹600. You are buying a growing business, not a static number. If the business grew 20% in that quarter, ₹600 today may be cheaper in real terms than ₹500 was three months ago.

### 2.2 The Crash Doubling Protocol — Enhanced

Your instinct to "double down during crashes" is correct, but it needs guardrails.

**Definition of a "Crash":**

| Condition | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Stock drops 10-15% in a week | **Normal volatility** | No action — this is routine |
| Stock drops 15-25% over 2-4 weeks | **Correction** | Review thesis — if intact, can accelerate next tranche |
| Stock drops 25-40% over 1-3 months | **Deep correction** | Full thesis revalidation — if intact, deploy additional tranche |
| Stock drops 40%+ | **Crash / Capitulation** | Deep revalidation + governance check — if everything is clean, deploy crash tranche |
| Market-wide drop (Nifty down 15%+) | **Market crash** | Deploy across multiple holdings — this is your best opportunity |

#### The Crash Revalidation Checklist

**Before doubling down on ANY crashed stock, verify ALL of the following:**

| # | Question | Must be YES to double down |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Is the company still profitable (or turning a corner)? | YES |
| 2 | Has the management addressed the cause of the crash in their communications? | YES |
| 3 | Is the crash driven by market-wide fear (not company-specific fraud/failure)? | YES |
| 4 | Is the competitive position unchanged or improved? | YES |
| 5 | Are promoters NOT selling shares during the crash? | YES |
| 6 | Is the debt situation unchanged or better? | YES |
| 7 | Do I still believe in this company for the next 5 years? | YES |

If ALL 7 are YES → **Double down with a "crash tranche" of ₹25,000 (or ₹50,000 for high-conviction holdings)**

If ANY is NO → **Do NOT double down.** Hold your existing position and wait for clarity.

#### The "Crash Tranche" Sizing

| Scenario | Extra Deployment |
|---|---|
| Market-wide crash + company fundamentals intact | Deploy 1 extra tranche (₹25,000) per holding that passes revalidation |
| Company-specific crash + fundamentals intact | Deploy 1 extra tranche (₹25,000) for that stock |
| Company-specific crash + fundamentals questionable | **Deploy nothing** — wait for next quarter |
| Company-specific crash + fundamentals deteriorating | **Consider exiting** entirely |

## 3. The Exit Protocol — When to Sell

Selling is harder than buying. Here is a rules-based framework to remove emotion from exit decisions.

### 3.1 The Primary Rule

**Sell when the reasons you bought no longer apply.** Not when the stock is down. Not when the market is panicking. Only when your investment thesis has fundamentally broken.

### 3.2 The Fundamental Deterioration Checklist

Monitor these quarterly. If 3+ of these turn red for 2 consecutive quarters, initiate exit.

| # | Metric | Warning Threshold | Exit Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Earnings growth** | Slowed below 10% for 1 quarter | Negative for 2 consecutive quarters |
| 2 | **Revenue growth** | Slowed below 5% for 1 quarter | Negative for 2 consecutive quarters |
| 3 | **ROCE** | Dropped below 15% | Dropped below 12% for 2 consecutive years |
| 4 | **Operating margins** | Declining for 2 quarters | Declining for 3+ quarters |
| 5 | **Debt/Equity** | Increased by > 0.3 in one year | Crossed above 1.5 |
| 6 | **Free cash flow** | Turned negative for 1 quarter | Negative for 3+ quarters (non-capex reason) |
| 7 | **Promoter holding** | Declined by > 2% in a quarter | Declined by > 5% in a year |
| 8 | **Promoter pledging** | Increased to > 15% | Increased to > 25% |
| 9 | **Management guidance** | Missed guidance for 1 quarter | Missed for 2 consecutive quarters |
| 10 | **Competitive position** | Losing market share | Structural disruption to business model |

### 3.3 The Exit Decision Matrix

| Warning Signals | Exit Signals | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 | 0 | **Continue holding** — business is healthy |
| 2-3 | 0 | **Heightened monitoring** — watch next quarter closely; pause accumulation |
| 3+ | 0 | **Stop accumulating** — do not add any more capital |
| Any | 1-2 | **Partial exit** — sell 30-50% of position |
| Any | 3+ | **Full exit** — sell entire position; thesis is broken |

### 3.4 Non-Fundamental Exit Triggers

| Trigger | Action |
|---|---|
| **Regulatory fraud discovered** (SEBI action, auditor resignation, financial restatement) | **Immediate full exit** — do not wait for clarification |
| **Promoter arrested / investigated** | **Immediate full exit** |
| **Extreme overvaluation** — PE > 2x 5-year median PE AND PEG > 3 | **Partial exit** — sell 50%; let rest ride with trailing stop |
| **Better opportunity** — you found a higher-conviction stock but are fully allocated | **Consider exiting lowest-conviction holding** to fund the new entry |
| **Position has become > 5% of portfolio** through appreciation | **Trim to 4%** — manage concentration risk |

### 3.5 What Is NOT a Reason to Sell

| NOT a Reason | Why You Should Hold |
|---|---|
| "The stock fell 20% this month" | Price drops without fundamental changes are opportunities, not exits |
| "The market is crashing" | If your thesis is intact, crashes are buying opportunities |
| "I saw a negative news article" | Verify if the article reflects actual fundamental changes; media is noisy |
| "My friend says it's bad" | Trust your own analysis over opinions without evidence |
| "I need the money for another stock" | Only sell if the other stock is genuinely higher conviction after full analysis |

## 4. Position Sizing & Portfolio-Level Rules

### 4.1 The Portfolio Construction Rules

| Rule | Threshold | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Maximum single stock** | 5% of portfolio at cost | No single bet should sink the portfolio |
| **Maximum per sector** | 25% of portfolio | Diversify across economic drivers |
| **Minimum number of stocks** | 15-20 | Sufficient diversification |
| **Maximum number of stocks** | 25-30 | Beyond 30, you are running an index fund with higher cost |
| **Cash reserve** | 5-10% of portfolio always in liquid | Ammunition for crash opportunities |

**Observation on your portfolio:** You currently hold 76 stocks. This is extremely diversified — potentially over-diversified. With 76 stocks at an average position of ₹63,500, your best ideas are diluted by many small positions. Consider gradually consolidating to 25-30 highest-conviction holdings.

### 4.2 When to Add a New Stock vs. Add to Existing

| Scenario | Action |
|---|---|
| You find a new stock scoring A on both quality and valuation | Add new stock; consider exiting lowest scorer |
| You find a new stock scoring B on both | Add only if portfolio has < 25 stocks |
| Existing stock had a great quarter | Add next tranche to existing (don't chase new ideas) |
| You're at 25+ stocks and find a new idea | Must exit something to maintain focus |
